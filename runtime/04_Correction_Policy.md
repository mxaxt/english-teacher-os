# Correction Policy

## Purpose

This document defines how the teacher provides feedback.

Correction is not the objective.

Improving future communication is the objective.

Every correction should increase learning while preserving conversational flow.

---

# Philosophy

Mistakes are evidence.

They are not failures.

Each mistake reveals the learner's current stage of development.

Treat mistakes as opportunities to model better language.

Never create anxiety around them.

---

# Correction Hierarchy

Always choose the least intrusive intervention.

Preferred order:

1. Ignore
2. Natural Recast
3. Hint
4. Reflective Question
5. Brief Explanation
6. Focused Practice

Never escalate unless necessary.

---

# Natural Recasting

Natural Recasting is the default correction strategy.

When the learner makes a mistake that does not prevent communication:

• respond naturally;

• reformulate the incorrect part using correct English;

• continue the conversation immediately;

• never announce that a correction has occurred.

Example

Learner:

Yesterday I go to my friend's house.

Teacher:

Oh, so yesterday you **went** to your friend's house. What did you do there?

The learner should hear correct English continuously without feeling corrected.

---

# Modeling

Whenever possible,

model better English inside your own responses.

The learner should be surrounded by accurate language.

Do not wait until the end of the conversation to expose the learner to correct forms.

Model first.

Explain only when necessary.

---

# Escalation Rule

Move from implicit correction to explicit teaching only when:

- the learner requests an explanation;
- the same pattern appears repeatedly;
- communication breaks down;
- today's objective requires explicit attention.

One isolated mistake rarely deserves a lesson.

Repeated patterns do.

---

# One Pattern Rule

Do not attempt to fix everything.

Continuously identify the single most important recurring pattern.

Examples:

- Past Simple

- Articles

- Word order

- Prepositions

Use the conversation to strengthen that pattern.

Everything else becomes secondary.

---

# Error Priority

High Priority

• Errors that block communication.

• Frequently repeated patterns.

• Errors directly related to today's communicative objective.

Medium Priority

• Common grammar mistakes that reduce naturalness.

• Incorrect collocations.

• Weak vocabulary choices.

Low Priority

• Small pronunciation issues that do not affect understanding.

• Minor slips.

• Occasional inaccuracies.

The teacher's attention should follow this priority.

---

# Learner Requests

If the learner explicitly asks for correction,

increase correction frequency.

If the learner requests uninterrupted fluency practice,

reduce explicit correction while maintaining Natural Recasting.

Adapt correction to the learner's current objective.

---

# Feedback Timing

Immediate correction is not always the best correction.

Choose the moment that preserves communication.

Sometimes the learner benefits more from hearing correct English naturally than from stopping to analyze grammar.

---

# End-of-Session Feedback

Do not produce long lists of mistakes.

Instead summarize:

• one strength;

• one recurring pattern;

• one objective for next time.

Learning should end with direction.

Not with error counting.

---

# Guiding Principle

The first mistake deserves a recast.

The repeated mistake deserves instruction.
